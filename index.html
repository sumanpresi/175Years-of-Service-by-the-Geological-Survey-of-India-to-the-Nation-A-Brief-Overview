Overview

The Geological Survey of India (GSI) stands as the revered 'Mother' institution for all geologists and Earth-related scientists in India. Founded on 4th March 1851 by Sir Thomas Oldham, GSI has been instrumental in India's geological exploration and mineral development for 175 years.

Foundation & Legacy

The arrival of Sir Thomas Oldham, Professor of Geology at Trinity College, Dublin, and Chief of the Irish Geological Survey at Calcutta on 4th March 1851, marked the beginning of GSI. From its inception, GSI has been the cornerstone of India's mineral exploration, leading to the establishment of numerous professional exploration agencies including AMD, ONGC, MECL, CGWB, and Coal India.

Age of Pioneers
1851 - 1900

1851: Thomas Oldham arrived in Calcutta, marking GSI's official founding.

1856: GSI's new office established at No.1, Hastings Street.

1857: First GSI Memoir on 'Talcher Coalfields' published by H.F. & W.T. Blanford.

Key Contributions: Introduction of new rock types including charnockite, khondalite, and the Gondwana System. R.D. Oldham's study of Assam Earthquake (1897) laid foundation of seismology in India.

Great Geologists & Industrialization
1901 - 1951

1901: Indian Mines Act introduced; Mining wing separated from GSI.

1906: Mining, Geological and Metallurgical Institute of India (MGMI) founded by T.H. Holland.

Coal Production: Rose from 10 million tonnes (1900) to 30 Mt (1930).

Post-Independence Advancement
1951 - 2001

1951: M.S. Krishnan became first Indian to head GSI. Centenary celebrations held.

1955: Separate Drilling Division started with 5 imported rigs (grew to 304 by 1971).

1961: Decentralization - GSI evolved from centrally administered to regional setup. B.C. Roy became first Director General.

1960s: Systematic Geological Mapping (SGM) national programme initiated. Formation of PSUs: NMDC (1961), BALCO (1965), HZL (1966), HCL (1967).

1965: Marine Geology Wing established.

1967: Airborne Geophysical Surveys began under 'Operation Hard Rock' (GOI-USAID collaboration).

1976: Training Institute started at Raipur (shifted to Hyderabad in 1986).

1981: Indian Scientific Expedition to Antarctica launched.

Systematic Geological Mapping (SGM)

Initiated as a national programme in the 1960s on 1:63,360/1:50000 scale. By 2001, approximately 98.23% of the total mappable area (3.14580 million km¬≤) was covered.

Engineering Geology & Ground Water Survey

Became separate divisions in 1957, providing geotechnical advice to major dams:

  • Damodar Valley Corporation
  • Bhakra Dam
  • Hirakud Dam
  • Beas-Sutlej Link Projects

Marine Geology & Coastal Survey

Systematic surveys began in 1983-1984 with procurement of ocean-going ships and coastal research vessels. India participated in the International Indian Ocean Expedition with 41 ships from 13 countries.

Airborne Surveys

  • 1967: Airborne Geophysical Surveys under 'Operation Hard Rock' (GOI-USAID)
  • 1970: Airborne Mineral Survey and Exploration (AMSE) started
  • Current: Multi-sensor aero-geophysical surveys (300m traverse line spacing at 80m above ground)

Antarctica Expedition

Started in 1981 with MV Polar Circle launch. GSI played leading role in 20+ multidisciplinary expeditions.

  • Dakshin Gangotri (1983-1984): India's first permanent wintering station
  • Maitri (1989): Second permanent station
  • Bharati (2012): Third station supporting wide range of scientific research

Meteorite Collection & Studies

GSI is the sole custodian and authorized curator of all meteorite falls on Indian soil. Currently possesses about 700 meteorites of different kinds. Virtual museum exhibits 105 Indian meteorites and 384 from worldwide.

Fossil Parks

  • Shivalik Fossil Park (Himachal Pradesh): Life-size vertebrate models from 1.5-2.5 million years ago
  • Mandla Plant Fossils National Park (Madhya Pradesh): Primordial forest remains from 40-150 million years ago
  • Dinosaurium, Hyderabad: Reconstructed Kotasaurus (14.5m long, 4.5m tall) from 840 skeletal parts at Birla Technological Museum
  • Additional parks in Bihar, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, Uttar Pradesh, and West Bengal

GSI Training Institute (GSITI)

Started at Raipur (1976), shifted to Hyderabad (1986). Provides state-of-the-art training with:

  • Headquarters at Hyderabad
  • Six centres: Hyderabad, Lucknow, Zawar, Ranchi, Raipur, Chitradurga
  • Specialized divisions: Photo Geology & Remote Sensing (PGRS), Geophysics, Centre for Geoinformation Management Training (CGMT)

Key Current Programs

National Geochemical Mapping (NGCM)

High-density sampling at 1km x 1km grid intervals analyzing 64 elements. Total coverage: 11.72 lakh km¬≤ by March 2020, including 8.13 lakh km¬≤ in Obvious Geological Potential (OGP) areas.

Applications: Identify mineral exploration areas, assess health hazards, assist agricultural research for higher crop yields.

National Aero Geophysical Mapping Program (NAGMP)

First-of-its-kind multi-sensor surveys (magnetic, gradiometric, spectrometric) with 300m traverse line spacing at 80m above ground level.

Results: Data acquisition over 8 blocks completed, carving out 100+ potential mineral exploration areas.

Coverage: Phase I (~8 lakh km¬≤) completed/under implementation; Phase II (~5 lakh km¬≤) proposed.

National Geoscience Data Repository (NGDR)

Flagship initiative to collate all geoscience data from GSI, national organizations, state DMGs, academia, CPSEs, and private sector.

Development: In consultation with BISAG-N (Ministry of Information & Technology).

Goal: Enable stakeholders globally to participate in mineral acreage allocation and ensure long-term mining viability.

National Landslide Forecasting Centre

Launched via 'bhusanket.gsi.gov' portal (19th July 2024). Major step towards disaster risk reduction with short-range and medium-range landslide forecasting.

Close evolution since 1958. Founder president Dr. D.N. Wadia and numerous GSI officers contributed to Society's growth. Notable collaborations include:

  • 10th International Kimberlite Conference (2012): Bengaluru with Dr. Fareeduddin as Organising Secretary
  • Publications: Books, memoirs, and field guides including "Diamonds and their Source Rocks in India" and "Southern Indian Kimberlites"

Employment Impact

Pioneering Publications

  • 2024: "REE Occurrences and Deposits of India with Strategies for new Discoveries" by P. Krishnamurthy

Geological Terminology Introduced

  • Charnockite: Hypersthene-bearing granite from Mt. Thomas, Chennai - named after Job Charnock (founder of Kolkata)
  • Khondalite: Garnet-sillimanite bearing high-grade metamorphic rocks from Eastern Ghats
  • Gondwana System: Introduced by H.B. Medlicott
